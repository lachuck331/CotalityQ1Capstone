Introduction

California wildfire risk assessment is difficult to operationalize at scale: most locations do not burn in a given month, positive fire events are extremely rare, and environmental conditions shift over time. Traditional simulation-heavy approaches can be expensive to run repeatedly across a statewide domain, while narrow local models may not transfer well to broader geography.

This project builds a reproducible statewide burn-probability pipeline on a harmonized 800m monthly grid with public climate, vegetation, terrain, landcover, and fire-perimeter data. The goal is decision support through calibrated risk ranking, with explicit temporal holdout evaluation to test real-world transfer under severe class imbalance.

🎯

Project Goals

Build a transparent statewide workflow that estimates monthly burn probability, prioritizes ranking quality for rare events, and provides interpretable signals for decision support.

⚙️

Prior Work

Compared with prior local-baseline efforts and non-temporal evaluations, this phase scales to statewide California, standardizes preprocessing across public geospatial sources, and adds tuned XGBoost with strict 2005–2009 temporal holdout testing.

🧱

Core Challenge

The target is a rare-event label with extreme class imbalance, so high accuracy can be misleading. Robust evaluation requires ROC-AUC/PR-AUC and temporal holdout testing, not accuracy alone.

🗺️

Statewide Scope

California domain at monthly resolution on a harmonized 800m grid; source coverage is 2000–2024, with model-ready records beginning in March 2000 after startup-missingness handling and lag alignment.
